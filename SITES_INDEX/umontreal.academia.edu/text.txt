Ancient Etymology and the Enigma of OkeanosRhizomata,  2020Okeanos is at once a mythological figure and a philosophical concept appearing in many ancient ac... more Okeanos is at once a mythological figure and a philosophical concept appearing in many ancient accounts of the world. A frequent object of allegoresis, his cosmological role and his name posed an enigma to Homer’s readers, especially those with a rationalizing bent. This paper proposes that the paradoxical representation of Okeanos as a primordial generative power and a geographical limit may be explained by the influence of etymological speculation, which was a popular heuristic method used by Greek intellectuals from the archaic period throughout antiquity.Save to LibraryEditCompare Citation Rank Readers Related Papers MentionsView Impact
Painting as an Aesthetic ParadigmThe Poetics in its Aristotelian Context, Edited by Pierre Destrée, Malcolm Heath and Dana Munteanu,  2020This chapter examines Aristotle’s references to visual arts and argues that the revolutionary the... more This chapter examines Aristotle’s references to visual arts and argues that the revolutionary theory presented in his Poetics may have been influenced by prior discussions of painting. Aristotle uses the paradigm of painting in three kinds of contexts: i. explicit analogies between poetry and painting that support theoretical principles of poetics; ii. implicit comparisons between their psychological and educational effects on their audience and their practitioners; iii. claims about their intrinsic value as mimetic arts. The explicit analogies between poetry and painting suggest that many puzzling features of Aristotle’s theorization of the former can be explained with reference to the model provided by the latter. The similarity between his assessment of the cognitive and moral value of painting and poetry also shows that painting was a constructive paradigm for his conception of mimetic crafts.Save to LibraryDownloadEditCompare Citation Rank Readers Related Papers MentionsView Impact
Aristarque et l’étymologie des épithètes divinesMuseum Helveticum,  2019On attribue au grammairien Aristarque plusieurs explications étymologiques portant sur des épithè... more On attribue au grammairien Aristarque plusieurs explications étymologiques portant sur des épithètes homériques arborées par des personnages divins dans l’épopée. Un examen de ces étymologies, ainsi qu’une comparaison avec des étymologies concurrentes, révèlent que l’intérêt d’Aristarque ne concerne pas tant la vraisemblance linguistique de ces étymologies (un concept d’ailleurs anachronique) que leur potentiel heuristique dans le cadre de son interprétation d’Homère. Dans la majorité des exemples concernés, l’étymologie proposée par Aristarque semble résulter de l’application d’une approche exégétique fondée sur le principe de la cohérence interne («expliquer Homère par Homère»). Épithètes discutées: Ἀλαλκομενηΐς, ἀκάκητα, Ἑλικώνιος, Λυκηγενής, κλυτόπωλος, ἤϊος, ἈργειφόντηςSave to LibraryEditCompare Citation Rank Readers Related Papers MentionsView Impact

Generic Ambiguity and “Tendentious” Humor in the Hellenistic EpigramArethusa,  2019This article looks at a selection of Hellenistic epigrams (mostly by Callimachus), focusing on th... more This article looks at a selection of Hellenistic epigrams (mostly by Callimachus), focusing on the way they produce humor through verbal ambiguity and subversion of the conventional form and meaning of epigrammatic topoi relating to religion, sexuality, common values and beliefs, and death.
