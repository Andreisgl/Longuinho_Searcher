The... moreDrawing on ethnographic data collected in a coastal village in southwestern Madagascar, the work explores the concept of memory through a study of contemporary hospitality practices aimed towards western tourists and other outsiders. The key observation is that memory is shaped by the interactions between two forms of colonial legacy. One is defined by the socially inscribed memory of past relations with powerful outsiders, mediated through myth and oral history. The other is defined by socially incorporated practices formed in the pre-colonial and colonial realms of the 19th and 20th centuries (and hence constitute a memory of such pasts in the present), and that today shift in the context of contact with western tourists and other foreigners. For the last decade, Ranomafana has been the scene of an intense international effort to conserve the rich species and ecosystem diversity at home in the region. In 1990, an integrated conservation/development project called the Ranomafana National Park Project (RNPP) was established. The goal of the RNPP is to link the conservation of Ranomafana area resources with the economic development of those resident Tanala peoples who, in reproducing their communities, threaten the continued existence of the ecosystems constituting the National Park. Thus, in exchange for the enclosure by the Park of what was once their traditional subsistence base, RNPP planners have offered to help Tanala communities define and satisfy their needs. This dissertation is concerned with the role of discourses on and about needs in linking resident Tanala to the extra-local markets, epistemologies, and values defining contemporary capitalism in the region. It is argued that through the contradictory forces that these discourses both mediate and are structured by, RNPP participants are coming to novel understanding of their needs and interests.
