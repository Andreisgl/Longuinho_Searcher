His retelling of Greek antiquity thus becomes a way to thematize Japan’s cultural crisis, look nostalgically at the past, and ideally to restore order in chaotic postwar society. Almost twenty years after Mishima’s work, Kurahashi rewrites the story of Medea in a contemporary Japanese context. Yet, unlike Mishima, she does not use ancient Greece to imbue traditional Japan with an idealized aura. Instead, she experiments with cross-cultural narrative forms that mirror the ambivalent role of a generation of Japanese intellectuals caught between Western literature, the avant-garde, and Japanese tradition. Moving from the analysis of the function of myth in Kurahashi’s literary work, this paper investigates how the mythological subtext redefines the boundaries of the traditional Japanese novel and contributes to the construction of Kurahashi’s “anti-world”. In Amanonkoku ōkanki, the myth of the Amazons–the barbarian opposite of Greek civilization–mirrors the reverse side of Japanese reality, exposing its incongruities and questioning the transparency of its language. Moreover, by reverting the traditional archetypes of the Greek hero and the Amazons, the novel plays with the construction of sexual identity and undermines the narrator’s point of view.Published in  Cogito – Multidisciplinary Journal 4 (1): 168-178.
