Chr. ), Neffe zweiten Grades der Kaiserin Mǝntǝwwab (ca. 1706-1773 n. Chr.), gehört mit seinem Vater Ǝšäte zu den wichtigen Figuren im Äthiopien des ausgehenden 18. Jahrhunderts. Reicher Besitz an Land und Immobilien erlauben ihm großzügige Kirchenstiftungen. In dem verwirrenden Spiel der regionalen Mächte seiner Zeit der Auflösung des Gondariner Königtums wechselt er dreimal, jeweils durch Heirat bekräftigt, die Allianzen und kann seine Macht als Provinzgouverneur (Däǧǧazmač) in Begämdǝr, östlich des Ṭana-Sees, mit Unterbrechungen durch Ungnade bei Hofe und Exil, halten, bis die Niederlage seines Schwiegervaters Däǧǧazmač Zäwde im Jahre 1809 n. Chr. auch seine politische Laufbahn beendet.Während eines Exils entsteht auf seine Anregung hin und durch die Feder seines begabten Sekretärs Abägaz aus Schoa eine umfangreiche Sammlung äthiopischer Chroniken, deren handschriftliche Überlieferung hier dargestellt ist. L’essai qui suit constitue la version remaniée de ma leçon inaugurale, ... more – Remarques préliminairesL’essai qui suit constitue la version remaniée de ma leçon inaugurale, prononcée le 15 novembre 2007 au Collège de France, dans le cadre de la Chaire Européenne 2007–2008 (Études coraniques). Un DVD comprenant le film intégral de la leçon inaugurale, un entretien de l’auteur avec le Professeur Michel Tardieu, une présentation par le Professeur Henry Laurens, et un livret accompagnant le coffret, est paru sous le titre Un philologue lit le Coran («Les leçons inaugurales du Collège de France», Paris: Collège de France – Cned – Doriane Films, 2008). La version imprimée de la leçon, dont la parution fut un temps annoncée aux Éditions Fayard, n’est en revanche jamais parue. En règle générale, les leçons inaugurales sont publiées sous forme d’ouvrage, les enregistrements vidéo, sous forme de DVD, paraissant seulement pour certaines leçons. Cela montre bien que la version papier conserve toujours sa légitimité.Save to LibraryDownloadEditCompare Citation Rank Readers Related Papers MentionsView Impact

The present ... more Comments as part of a collective work on 50 selected sūras, explained in the volume:The present volume is the work of 25 scholars who participated in the 2012–13 Notre Dame Qurʾān Seminar. The Qurʾān Seminar scholars represent various specializa- tions important to the study of the Qurʾān, including Arabic language, comparative Semitic linguistics, paleography, epigraphy, history, rhetorical theory, hermeneutics, and Biblical studies. The 2012 – 13 Qurʾān Seminar project involved five conferences, each of which consisted of a series of ten sessions, with each session dedicated to a passage of the Qurʾān. After those conferences the editors of this work solicited writ- ten commentaries from the Qurʾān Seminar scholars. in old South Arabia inscriptions. The so-called expiation or confession texts in old South Arabia inscriptions duly attracted the attention of scholars in the field of comparative history of religion. As public and seemingly spontaneous confessions of religious offences, mostly situated in the sphere of ritual purity, by individuals, the parallels to Judaism and Islam are obvious. Nonetheless the element of spontaneous public and written confession struck the mind of the scholars who accepted all too willingly this interesting "anomaly" in religious history. Recently discovered texts from Raybun (Hadramaut) belonging to the same genre are longer than usual, more detailed and definitely treat misdeeds and offences in the field of social, if not economic life. They tell us of offenders and (human) offended, possibly witnesses and mediators or judges appear, one hears of trial (which implies plaintiffs and defendants), decision and fine. There are all elements of a legal procedure. This leads to a different hermeneutical approach to them as stereotyped judicial documents of Yemen. Perhaps judicial courts were attached to the temples, the priests themselves acted as judges. The role of the "published" cases could well have been that of exemplary precedents or a kind of pillory. The expressions used in the stereotype formulas of these texts would then belong to the vast realm of political and religious euphemisms: "To confess" would cover: "to be denounced, interrogated", "to be humiliated" would mean "to be judged and condemned" etc. The paper tries to show the totally different understanding by applying these premises to some chosen samples of old South Arabian inscriptions expiation texts.Save to LibraryEditCompare Citation Rank Readers Related Papers MentionsView Impact
