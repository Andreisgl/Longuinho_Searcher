MelenaDownload Free PDFView PDFContacto cultural entre el Mediterráneo y el  …Carros micénicos del Heládico Final III en las estelas decoradas del Bronze Final II-IIIA del Suroeste de la Península Ibérica2008 • Alfredo Mederos MartinThe social importance of the chariots is evident from beginnings of the second millennium BC in the Near East and the Aegean, in military parades and ceremonial processions, and reveals that its iconography is directly associated with the royalty. For this reason, the chariot was the best reflex to the social inequality during the Late Bronze Age, between all the objects of the warriors represented in the decorated stelae. The daily functionality of the chariot was the rapid and comfortable displacement of one or two persons and its occasional employment in the hunt. The military use of the chariot was the same that in the Aegean, employment in parades, where the height of the chariot permitted to the driver increased his height over the warriors on foot; the rapid displacement of the leader to the place of the combat and after to fight on foot, and the possible withdrawal of the injured leader during the combat, avoiding the demoralization of the troops. The arrival of the first chariots to the Iberian Peninsula was during the Late Helladic IIIB, ca. 1325-1185 BC, parallel to the Late Bronze IIA-IIB, ca. 1325/1300-1150 BC, and it was contemporary with the appearance of the leaf-shaped swords in Iberia, also represented in some stelae. The generalization of the chariot in inner Iberia was stimulated by the likely distribution of the Mycenaean craters with chariot decoration to be used in banquets. These Mycenaean craters with chariots decoration were largely exported, being very requested for its incorporation in the funeral apparels of the Cyprus tombs, but not in Ugarit or in the own Mycenaean Greece, what suggests that this mode of representing chariots was brought to the Iberian Peninsula with the intervention of Cypriot sailors and merchants.
