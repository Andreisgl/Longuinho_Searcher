These differences are rooted in the widely divergent religious contexts and the different characters of the peoples for whom each philosopher is, in a way, legislating. Montesquieu finds in the early legal practices of the Germanic and Frankish barbarians certain legal institutions which he opposes to the innovations of Roman law under the influence of classical political thought and Christianity. In this way he diverges from other modern political theorists who make independent philosophic arguments concerning the natural rights to life, liberty, and property to oppose to the justifications for inherited political forms. Instead, Montesquieu points to customary, protean forms of law among the barbarians which are rooted in the passion for vengeance, the desire to feel secure and independent, and the intractability of a warlike, free, and pastoral people. By showing that premodern views of law combined with the Christian concern for belief and tendency toward monarchy or despotism are largely incompatible with these conditions and must therefore defer to the preexisting forms of the sacred and customary, he reflects Plato in his Laws more than he resembles modern imposers of rational, institutional systems.
