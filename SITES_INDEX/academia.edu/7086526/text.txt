ABSTRACT: While the book of 1 Maccabees presents letters that would have been exchanged between Jews and Spartans (1 Macc. 12:6-18, 20-23; 14:20-23) and a praise of the Romans (1 Mac. 8:1-16), these two peoples, who are related in 1 Maccabees (14:16), also appear in the account of 2 Maccabees, which brings a letter from the Romans to the Jews (2 Mac. 11:34-38) and mentions an ethnic relationship between Jews and Spartans (2 Mac. 5:9). The present article seeks to analyze such indications of relations between Jews, Spartans and Romans in the books of 1 Maccabees and 2 Maccabees from their context within the Hellenistic world and the culture of Hellenism, that was dominant, by studying the Greek terms used to express such relations in order to determine what these texts suggest and their relation to their historical context. From Seleucus I to Antiochus IV (305-164 B.C.)]2015 • Aida Fernández PrietoResumen: Todo régimen político, pasado y presente, ha desarrollado diferentes mecanismos para favorecer su aceptación y, con ello, su supervivencia. Las monarquías helenísticas no fueron una excepción a la norma; sumándose, además, el complejo y precario escenario en el que estas habían ido emergiendo. Este ensayo aborda los instrumentos que una de dichas dinastías, la Seléucida, empleó en aras de que su gobierno resultara admisible y, por ende, tolerable, no solo ante las variadas poblaciones de su propio imperio, sino ante los ojos de otros estados rivales. Genuinos receptores de la herencia de Alejandro, líderes militares victoriosos, descendientes de Apolo, benefactores, fundadores de ciudades, etc.; fueron algunas de las formas en las que se presentaron los primeros reyes del Imperio Seléucida (305-164 a.C.). En ocasiones, el objetivo fue cumplido; en otros, sin embargo, la recepción de la imagen del monarca sería del todo negativa. Abstract: Any political regimen, past or present, has developed different mechanisms to promote its acceptance and, thus its survival. The Hellenistic monarchies were not an exception to the rule, adding to the complex and precarious scenario in which they had been emerging. This essay addresses the instruments that one of these dynasties, the Seleucids kings, used in order that their government would be admissible and, therefore tolerable, not only for the various populations of their own empire, but also in the eyes of their rival states. Being considered genuine receptors of the heritage of Alexander, victorious military leaders, descendants of Apollo, benefactors, founders of cities, etc.; were some of the ways in which these first kings of the Seleucid Empire (305-164 BC) presented themselves.
