It opens with a survey of the relevant secondary literature and is divided into three chapters: 1. the first is devoted to the origins of the Chaldean oracles, their transmission through the works of Proclus, Michael Psellus and Georgios Gemistos, their reception in Renaissance thought by Ficino and Pico, and the different kinds of sources from which Pico derived what he considered to be the Zoroastrian, Chaldean and Persian doctrines; 2. the second chapter offers an examination of Oratio §§ 43-44 and considers the possible derivation of the source used by Pico in this passage from one of Michael Psellos’ sketches of the Chaldean doctrines; 3. the third chapter brings evidence to the hypotheses that the author of the “Chaldean” texts used by Pico was influenced by Pico’s own thought and that the conception of man put forward by Pico in his Oratio is strongly related to Menaem of Recanati’s Commentary on the Torah. On the basis of the circumstantial evidence gathered in the previous chapters, the following conclusions are formulated: 1. that the narrative concerning the creation of man contained in Oratio §§ 10-23 is in fact an allegoric transposition of the anagogic interpretation of Genesis 1.26 and Genesi 5.1-2 put forward by Recanati in his Commentary on the Torah on the basis of passages drawn from the Zohar and the Bahir, and 2. that the Chaldean texts studied by Pico must have been written by the very person who had translated for Pico Recanati’s Commentary on the Torah – Guglielmo Raimondo Moncada alias Flavius Mithridates.Download Free PDFView PDFExil und Heimatferne in der Literatur des Humanismus von Petrarca bis zum Anfang des 16.
