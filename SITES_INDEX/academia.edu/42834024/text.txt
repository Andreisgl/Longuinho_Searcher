History and sociology of international quantification)-- Habilitation à diriger des recherches-HDRRoser Cussó2012, Sciences Po-Paris, 25 November 2012, Paris. http://www.sudoc.fr/182633624One of the most outstanding undertakings of quantification, in terms of volume, diversity and impact, is the system set up by intergovernmental and supranational organizations (IOs). The influence of international quantification on public policies in the South is well known –the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) were an example. The fact that surveys, such as the Program for International Student Assessment (PISA) of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) or instruments such as of the European Union (EU) benchmarking, are also capable of having an impact on Northern countries has helped to increase interest in IO statistical programs as a global phenomenon. Less work has been done to study their origin and specificity, not to mention the historical and sociological analysis of international quantification – a scientific field in its own right. This dissertation for the HDR (qualification for supervising research), undertakes the theorization of the disruption introduced by IO statistics; first, as regards international activity on data comparability in the nineteenth century (International Statistical Congresses, 1853-1876, International Statistical Institute, since 1885) and, second, as regards statistics produced by States. The two distinctions here, historical and institutional, lead to the new direction of research that is proposed. Indeed, IO statistics, as a political instrument, derive from the introduction, in the twentieth century, of the formal link between three political spheres: international, transnational and intergovernmental. This linkage based approach allows for new applications of quantification analysis to issues that eluded simple explanation – i.e. where just scaling up the “mise en discipline” of statistics of States to the international level did not suffice. IOs, like states, are both political entities and knowledge producers. Yet, being devoid of classical democratic foundations, they directly connect knowledge to ethics without pluralistic mediation (majority/opposition, for example). In this context, quantified cross-country comparisons not only support the normative nature of IOs – to standardize, recommend, and regulate – but also provide the raison d’être of these organizations. While the power of modern states (army, taxes…) is reinforced by statistics, IOs largely exist through their quantification and related technical cooperation aspects. Finally, by being directly based on ethical goals, considered as universal, such quantification and cooperation ultimately influence the political functioning of states. The “at source” harmonization of country statistical methods and categories is a notable example. As for the case studies contained in this dissertation, they concern the evolution of statistical outputs of several IOs (League of Nations, UN, World Bank, UNESCO...) as well as the study of different quantified domains (demography, education, economy...). The definition of the stages of international quantification is one of the main contributions of this thesis. They allow one to discuss the history and sociology of IO quantification. For the interwar period, several domains of quantification under the LoN, the first political IO, have been analyzed, notably, the supervision of mandates, framing the first “development policies”, and the economic and financial activities, encouraging freedom of trade, both based on technical cooperation and cross-country comparison. After World War II, the World Bank's macroeconomic policies and related macro-demographic studies, on the one hand, and UNESCO’s early statistics on education, on the other, illustrate the blooming of development policies in their Keynesian phase. From the 1980s, the neoliberal economic turning point was reflected in the demographic data of the World Bank, focused on the measurement of demographic behaviors, in tune with the accession of microeconomics. Benchmarking is being developed by both the World Bank and the EU, consolidating a new globalization of international quantification and IO instruments. In the current post-globalization phase, education statistics already show signs of increasing polarization and regionalization: OECD, UNESCO, UNICEF and the World Bank are proposing quite different indicators. Statistical techniques also evolved throughout these periods, relaying through descriptive statistics, probabilistic, and big data approaches (algorithms integrating flexible methods). Finally, concerning methodology of research, this dissertation proposes a theoretical framework for the analysis of international quantification for sociologists, historians and political scientists with competences or interests relating to the technical dimension of data. The preferred methods include the study of the archives of IOs, both older and more recent (analysis of institutions, actors, data, exchanges, practices...), and the analysis of official texts and discourses. Does the link between IOs and their data mirror the relationship between States and their statistics, or should the emergence of something new be taken into account? To attempt to answer these questions we study the origins of modern statistics (the Statistical Society of Paris, SSP) as well as International Congresses of Statistics in the second half of the nineteenth century. From the outset, French statisticians refer to the international dimension of their discipline, while the Congresses show a clear intention to promote the comparability of data. However, this comparability is erratic and the SSP’s activity remains focused on the link between the State and its quantification. This contrast between intentions and results enlightens both the priority of the reinforcement of State (harmonized) statistics and the fact that international data would only develop with the creation of the first political IO in 1919.
