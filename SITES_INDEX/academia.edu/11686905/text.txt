This site has been continuously inhabited throughout the 3rd and 1st millennium BC and the excavations directed by Prof. P. E. Pecorella in Area G have revealed an archaeological sequence allowing us to provide additional evidence to understand both the changes in ceramic manufacture and the transformation of the settlement that took place during the second half of 2nd millennium. The paper presents an overview of pottery dated to the Late Bronze Age period. On the basis of stratigraphic observations we have been able to divide our Late Bronze Age phase into two main levels: the earliest level highlights the presence of painted and grey wares next to a varied repertoire of common ware types, whereas the second and more recent level is characterised by a common and coarser production. The presented data demonstrate that the pottery assemblage of the earliest period should place Tell Barri within a local envi- ronment having some links with both northern and western encounters, while, during the more recent period, the evidence principally go back to the east- ern regions. The hypothesis that here we would like to argue is that different cultural traditions are working during the Late Bronze Age, contributing to differentiate the two ceramic assemblages. The proposed explanation suggests that variation in ceramic culture reflects mainly socio-economic factors tightly bound to the new political course inaugurated by Assyrians.
