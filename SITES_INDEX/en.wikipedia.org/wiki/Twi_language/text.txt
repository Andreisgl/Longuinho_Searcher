Twi ([tɕᶣi]) is a dialect of the Akan language spoken in southern and central Ghana by several million people, mainly of the Akan people, the largest of the seventeen major ethnic groups in Ghana. Twi has about 17–18 million speakers in total, including second-language speakers; about 80% of the Ghanaian population speaks Twi as a first or second language.[4][2] Twi has a simple tonal language system which is reflected in its phonology.[5]
Twi is a common name for mutually intelligible former literary dialects of the Akan language, Bono, Asante, and Akuapem.[6][7][4] Akuapem, as the first Akan dialect to be used for Bible translation, has become the prestige dialect as a result.[8] It is also spoken by the people of southeastern Côte d'Ivoire.[9][7][10]
Etymology[edit]
The name "Twi" is derived from the name of a Bono king, Nana Baffuor Twi.[11]

Tone[edit]
Twi has at least 5 tones:
High tone: H
Mid tone: M
Low tone: L
Rising tone: R
Falling tone: F
However, when writing Twi using the Latin script, tone marks are not used.
Diphthongs[edit]
Twi contains the diphthongs /ao/, /eɛ/, /ei/, /ia/, /ie/, /oɔ/, /ue/, and /uo/.[12]

Naming system[edit]
The Akan peoples use a common Akan (Ghana) naming system of giving the first name to a child, based on the day of the week that the child was born. Almost all the tribes and clans in Ghana have a similar custom.
